Slide 3: Herman Hollerith
American inventor and statistician Herman Hollerith developed the punched card tabulating machine, revolutionizing data processing and contributing significantly to the field of computing. His invention, crucial for the 1890 U.S. Census, eventually led to the founding of IBM. Hollerith's work provided a foundation for modern computing systems.
Slide 4: Vannevar Bush
Vannevar Bush, an American engineer and inventor, contributed to analog computing, the Manhattan Project, and theorized about future information technology. His essay "As We May Think" envisioned hypertext and the World Wide Web, showing remarkable foresight into the digital age.
Slide 5: Turing Machine
The Turing Machine, a theoretical model proposed by Alan Turing in 1936, is a cornerstone in the theory of computation. It laid the groundwork for the digital computer, demonstrating that any computable task could theoretically be performed by a machine.
Slide 6: Automatic Computing Engine (ACE)
The ACE, designed by Alan Turing, was an early electronic stored-program computer. It demonstrated key concepts of modern computing, such as the use of a high-level programming language and innovative features that influenced subsequent computer development.
Slide 7: The Z3
Designed by Konrad Zuse in 1941, the Z3 was a pioneering computer that utilized automatic programming, marking it as an important step towards modern computing. It was capable of performing arithmetic and logical operations with floating-point numbers.
Slide 8: Harvard Mark I
The Harvard Mark I, also known as the ASCC, was a major advancement in automatic digital computing. Developed at Harvard University with IBM's support, it performed vital calculations for the US Navy during WWII and demonstrated the feasibility of large-scale automatic computing.
Slide 9: ENIAC
The ENIAC was one of the earliest electronic general-purpose computers. Developed for military calculations, it marked a significant leap forward in computing technology, paving the way for the digital age.
Slide 10: John von Neumann and EDVAC
John von Neumann's theoretical work was crucial for the development of the EDVAC, one of the earliest stored-program computers. His concept of a stored-program architecture became a fundamental aspect of modern computer design.
